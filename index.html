Writing Without a Boss: An Interview with Anders, Founder of Haddock Press

Background

Born in 1959, Anders-Peter Mathiasen initially studied at Aarhus University as a history major from 1979 to 1982. After graduating, Anders decided to pursue his passion for journalism, which he studied at the Danish School of Media and Journalism, graduating in 1986. Anders then went on to spend nearly two decades as a journalist and editor for various influential media corporations around Denmark, including Ekstra Bladet, Politiken, Jyllands-Posten and DR (Danish Broadcasting Corporation).

During Anders' career, Anders became known for his investigative journalism. Anders won the prestigious Cavling Prize, the highest honor in Danish journalism, in 1989 and finally in 2004. Over the course of his career, he would go on to author and co-author numerous books covering everything from organized crime to Danish political scandals. Among his works are Dan Lynge, Mit dobbeltliv, Blekingegadebanden, Politiets hemmeligheder, and Den danske underverden.

Perhaps Anders' most famous work is Det Bedste Bud (The Best Offer), which was published by People's Press. The book covers Anders' investigation of the controversial 2014 sale of Dong Energy shares to Goldman Sachs, a deal which was pushed by finance minister Bjarne Corydon.

After spending decades working for others, Anders wanted his own freedom. In 2005, he founded his own publishing and production company called Haddock Press, along with a cofounder. The company then operated successfully for years until Anders decided to shut it down, along with his retirement two years ago.

When I met with Anders during core course week, he reflected not only on the professional aspect of his journey, but also the lessons he learned regarding freedom, creativity and responsibility.

Interview

Q1: What's your story before starting Haddock Press, and what made you want to become an entrepreneur?

Anders explained that while there were many reasons as to why he wanted to start his own publication, the main reason was simple. "At some point, I realized I didn't want bosses anymore," he told me.

Instead of choosing to climb the journalism hierarchy, Anders chose the freedom of independence. "Becoming my own boss meant I could choose what stories to tell and how to tell them," he explained.

Q2: What was the initial idea for your business?

The original vision for Haddock Press was a wide range of services. Anders and his cofounder wanted an agency that was broad enough to cover not just journalism stories, but also tv production, books, and other various forms of journalism that weren't covered by traditional media. "We wanted to do a broad kind of journalism," Anders expressed.

The company was less about rigid structure, and more about the adaptability of being able to create whatever form of media he wished to build. And perhaps most importantly of all, "to not have bosses".

Q3: Has your business idea changed over time? What is the current business model or mission?

"Honestly, not much changed," Anders admitted. Unlike many other startups that pivot, the unique edge that Anders and his cofounder had were their already established reputations within the journalism world. "Because of the reputation we had in our line of work, people often came to us with the work," Anders explained.

Their mission remained the same throughout, telling stories in ways that reflected their own personal voices, unswayed by others. Sometimes Anders worked alone, other times he collaborated with cofounders or partners, depending on the project.

Q4: What was the hardest part of starting your business?

Without hesitation, Anders laughed and said: "Taxes."

The biggest challenge wasn't writing books, securing interviews, or producing content, it was the bureaucracy.

For someone who was so skilled in his craft, Anders found managing the administrative side of entrepreneurship to have the steepest learning curve. When it came to creating journalistic content, it was something he was already very familiar with.

Q5: How did you make your product/service stand out?

Anders credited his and his partner's personal reputations as the main differentiator. "We had a unique edge because of our personalities," he remarked.

While most other businesses would seek out their clients, in Anders' case, clients sought him and his company out because they trusted him and his partners' services. "People came to us because of who we were, not just what we produced," he said.

Q6: What are the most important lessons you've learned from starting your own business?

"The most important lesson was to fight for myself," Anders said. "When you're an employee, you get your money every month and you don't have to think about anything besides projects," he explained.

While some might like this life, Anders remarked that he found the monotony of this life boring. "It's like jumping from break to break," he explained, like life was an endless cycle of starting and ending projects, waiting for the next holiday, and then repeating the cycle for eternity.

In stark contrast, the life of an entrepreneur is riskier. You never know how much you're getting paid, and you never know when the next paycheck is coming in. Through this, Anders learned an important lesson: to always advocate for himself, whether it be through negotiating contracts or interacting with clients.

Q7: What's one piece of advice you would give yourself if you could go back in time to when you first began your business?

"Believe in it," Anders said immediately.

Looking back, he considers himself fortunate that Haddock Press grew smoothly. "I was quite happy, and also pretty lucky," he reflected.

Reflection and Takeaways

Interviewing Anders was a reminder to me of the different types of entrepreneurship present in our world. When I think of entrepreneurship, what often comes to mind is either f&b, tech or a finance company and so I found it extremely interesting to hear about Anders' journalism agency.

Another aspect I found interesting was the story that Anders had was vastly different from the other entrepreneurs we met during core course week. Unlike them, Anders never iterated on his business, nor did he have to actively seek out clients. It was a powerful reminder that sometimes, if a product or service is good enough, the product or service itself will generate its own marketing.

For me, Anders' journey emphasises the idea that not every entrepreneurial journey is the same. For some, like Anders', the company never pivoted, nor did it have to chase growth. Haddock Press remained consistent during its operation due to its founders' reputation, and because they stayed true to their principles and mission.

More broadly though, Anders' reflections still apply to any entrepreneur. Be ready to do the "boring" work, such as taxes, be ready to fight for yourself, and be ready to live a riskier life. Most importantly, trust in yourself, and believe in the vision you have for your business. In doing so, one unlocks the freedom to tell their own story, in their own way.
